# Ontology Blueprint V1 — DeepSeek Review

## Summary

The blueprint is well-argued and evidence-anchored — the run-3 data makes the D1 case overwhelmingly, and ¶419 gives D2 a clear north star. I agree with all three leans in direction but have one substantive departure and one hedge recommendation. **D1: A, high confidence** — the run-3 evidence is dispositive. **D2: C with a critical hedge on predicate-vocabulary size** (the lean's direction is right; the risk profile needs narrowing). **D3: D, medium confidence** — gating T2 seeds while leaving T3 neighbors open is the superior tradeoff at current scale; domain-as-coordinate (C) becomes the right answer at scale but D protects Discover earlier. The node/edge inventory is complete; the Claim↔LINKS_TO division of labor is coherent but introduces a query indirection cost the blueprint under-weights.

## D1 — Domain model

- **Pick: A** · **Confidence: high**
- **Reasoning:** The run-3 evidence makes this near-dispositive. Pass-1 classifies 29 sources into 11 domains reliably; Pass-2 emits only 4 domains across 185 pages, missing `value-investing` entirely — its largest source bucket. The blueprint's reframe (Source has one domain as a property; Entity has many as edges) is clean and maps to the data. Deriving `BELONGS_TO` from `SUPPORTS` + `Source.domain` gives 100% coverage, zero LLM cost, full recomputability, and a straightforward backfill path for run-3 validation. The "against" in option A (inherited, not intrinsic domain) is a feature, not a bug — under Philosophy B, the source's classification *is* the authority. An entity's domain should reflect who discusses it, not what it "intrinsically is" (which is what the failed Pass-2 tried and couldn't do). Option B re-introduces the LLM to solve a problem the data says doesn't need an LLM; option C breaks the viewer and D3's per-compile domain reads. A is the only option that solves coverage now without adding complexity.
- **What we missed:** Multi-domain entity semantics. An entity discussed by 5 value-investing sources and 1 AI source gets `BELONGS_TO` edges to both domains. This is correct, but consumers (D3 weighting, domain analytics) need to know that one domain is *dominant* and the other is *incidental*. The blueprint should note that the conferring-source count (see below) naturally produces a dominance signal — no separate mechanism needed, just document it as the intended interpretation.
- **Sub-questions:**
  - **`via_source` — record it, as a count.** Storing a `conferring_source_count INT64` (or a JSON array of source_ids) on `BELONGS_TO` is cheap and makes the edge self-explanatory. A query for "why is Buffett in value-investing?" should not require a `SUPPORTS` join. It also directly feeds D3 weighting: entities strongly anchored in a domain (high count) get higher same-domain priority. The join-back-through-`SUPPORTS` alternative works but adds latency to every domain-scoped read.
  - **`sub_domain` — retire it.** Under D1-A there is no per-entity sub-domain source. The attribute today carries Pass-2 page-domain values that are already broken. Keeping a nullable column with no population path is technical debt. Retire in the D1 migration.

## D2 — Claim layer

- **Pick: C (hedged)** · **Confidence: medium**
- **Reasoning:** I agree with the lean's architecture — wire `Claim` + `EVIDENCES` + `ABOUT` now, defer `SUPERSEDES`/`CONTRADICTS`/`QUALIFIES` to the gated #83/#84 arc. The split along the Relate/Learn boundary is clean and respects the Round 6 contract. The direction is right. My concern is a failure mode the blueprint names but doesn't fully hedge against: **predicate-vocabulary fragmentation.** The Claim schema has `predicate_class_canonical` — a controlled relationship vocabulary — but the blueprint doesn't specify how large or domain-specific that vocabulary should be. If the extraction prompt tries to emit investing-specific predicates (`has_moat`, `compounds_capital`) for investing sources, AI-specific predicates (`uses_attention_mechanism`) for AI sources, and health-specific predicates for health sources, the result will be dozens of low-frequency, domain-bound predicates that fragment the Claim layer into silos — exactly the "noisy, low-yield" failure mode the panel is asked to assess. **Recommendation: start with a domain-general predicate vocabulary of 5–8 relations.** Candidates: `asserts` (the source claims X about the subject), `exemplifies` (the subject is an instance/example of the object), `contrasts_with` (the subject is distinguished from the object), `depends_on` (the subject relies on the object), `is_analogous_to` (structural similarity across domains), `causes` / `enables` (causal or enabling relation). These cross domains naturally: "Mr. Market" `exemplifies` behavioral bias (investing↔psychology); Sleep `enables` cognitive performance (health↔neuroscience). A small, domain-general vocabulary keeps extraction tractable, supports cross-domain reasoning, and avoids the fragmentation trap. If 5–8 predicates prove high-quality at personal scale, expand. If they don't, the whole idea was unworkable anyway. Call this **C-hedged**: wire the schema but gate the predicate vocabulary size in the extraction prompt.
- **Personal-scale value of claim extraction:** At ~30 sources / ~180 entities, I'm cautiously optimistic IF the predicate vocabulary stays small and domain-general. The risk profile shifts sharply with vocabulary size: at 5 predicates, extraction is a classification task (which of these 5 relations, if any, best describes this assertion?); at 20+, it becomes open-ended relation extraction, which is research-grade and unreliable at this scale. The blueprint's ¶407 honesty applies here too — claim extraction at personal scale is useful as a *structured note-taking scaffold*, not a reasoning engine. That's worth building. Just don't oversell it.
- **Claim↔LINKS_TO division of labor:** Coherent in principle. LINKS_TO is cheap, high-recall associative adjacency; Claim is expensive, high-precision typed assertion. They serve different rungs (Relate-by-association vs Relate-by-reasoning). One concern the blueprint under-weights: **query disambiguation.** When a consumer asks "how are A and B related?", it gets both a bare `LINKS_TO` edge and a typed Claim — two answers at different semantic levels with no explicit relationship between them. The consumer needs a convention: either Claims *imply* a `LINKS_TO` (so the bare edge is redundant for claimed pairs), or they're independent layers. The blueprint should state the intended convention. I'd recommend: Claim creation does NOT auto-create a `LINKS_TO`; consumers that want both merge at query time. This keeps the layers clean and avoids edge-count inflation.

## D3 — T2/T3 domain-scoping

- **Pick: D** · **Confidence: medium**
- **Reasoning:** I depart from the assistant's lean of C here. At current scale (~180 entities, 11 domains), the critical-density problem (§7.4b) is not acute — the graph isn't saturated enough for "everything connects to everything." The immediate risk is the opposite: a hard T2 gate that silences cross-domain links before they can form. D (gate T2 seeds, open T3 neighbors) gives you the best of both worlds at this scale: T2 — where `entity_search_keys` matching is fuzzy and same-slug ambiguities are real — gets the precision of domain gating. T3 — the 1-hop neighbor expansion — stays open, so a value-investing entity with a `LINKS_TO` a psychology entity pulls that cross-domain context into the compile window. This protects Discover without sacrificing T2 precision. C (domain-as-coordinate) is the *asymptotically* correct answer — at scale, same-domain weighting will be essential — but at current scale it's an optimization you don't yet need, and D preserves the cross-domain path more aggressively during the formative runs where edge vocabulary is still being built. C can be dialed in later as a weighting overlay on top of D's architecture without reversing the decision.
- **What we missed:** The blueprint doesn't address **hub suppression** as a companion to domain scoping. Popular entities (Buffett, Claude, AI, value-investing as a topic) will dominate context budgets at scale regardless of domain, because they have high degree and appear in many T1/T2/T3 sets. Domain weighting (under C) or tiered gating (under D) helps but doesn't solve the "Buffett gets 40% of every compile window" problem. Degree-based penalties or a hub-exclusion list should be in the D3 scope — even as a deferred note — because domain and degree are orthogonal axes of context-budget pressure.
- **Sub-questions:**
  - **Weighting aggressiveness (if C):** Start at 70/30 same-domain/cross-domain budget split, tunable. This is aggressive enough to deliver precision but leaves meaningful room for serendipity. Monitor cross-domain Claim emergence as the canary — if cross-domain `LINKS_TO` edges exist but never produce Claims, the budget may be too tight.
  - **Gate-seeds-open-neighbors vs uniform:** Under my pick (D), gated T2 + open T3 is the right structure. The rationale: seed precision matters more because a wrong seed cascades; neighbor expansion is inherently recall-oriented and domain-gating it kills Discover. If the panel converges on C instead of D, the weighting should be applied uniformly (no tiering within C — it adds complexity without clear benefit at current scale).

## Cross-cutting (inventory + frame)

- **Node/edge inventory:** Complete. No missing node or edge type. The rationale for each entry is tied to a specific rung, which makes the inventory auditable. One minor note: the `Source` node's `author` and `summary` fields are listed as intrinsic metadata but the blueprint doesn't say whether they're populated today. If they're still NULL for most sources (Pass-1 enrichment may not have backfilled them), that's a data-completeness gap, not an inventory gap.
- **Frame compliance:** The three decisions all stay within the settled frame. D1-A is pure B (structure-derived, not LLM-imposed); D2-C splits at the Relate/Learn boundary while respecting the #83 gating; D3-D keeps domain as coordinate (T2) and avoids the coordinate→gate drift the settled frame warns against.
- **Claim reification cost (¶419 bridge):** The blueprint pitches Claim as the answer to ¶419, but ¶419 asks for typed entity-to-entity relations. Claim delivers *reified* relations (Source→Claim→Entity), which adds an indirection hop. This is justified — you can't attach confidence, provenance, polarity, and version to a bare edge — but the most common consumer query ("how are A and B related?") now requires traversing `(A)←[:ABOUT]-(Claim)-[:ABOUT]→(B)` rather than a direct typed edge. The blueprint should acknowledge this as a deliberate tradeoff (reification buys Learn-readiness at the cost of query complexity) rather than presenting Claim as a drop-in replacement for typed edges. It's the right tradeoff — just make it explicit.

## Convergence note

I expect strong panel convergence on D1-A (the run-3 evidence is hard to argue with). D2 will be the most contested — I'd bet at least one reviewer pushes for B (defer entirely, ship run-4 fast) and another pushes for A (go all-in on the ¶419 bet). The C-with-predicate-hedge position I'm staking is a middle ground that might attract consensus. D3 will likely split between C and D, with the scale argument being the deciding factor. The hub-suppression point (cross-cutting) is a load-bearing catch I'd expect at least one other reviewer to raise independently.
